Type of Schizophrenia and treatment

Type of Schizophrenia
Paranoid schizophrenia

  • This is the most common type of schizophrenia
  • develop later in life than other forms
  • Symptoms include hallucinations, delusions, but speech and emotions may not be affected

Type of Schizophrenia
Hebephrenic schizophrenia

  • AKA disorganized schizophrenia
  • Typically develops at 15-25 years old
  • Symptoms include disorganized behaviors and thoughts, alongside short-lasting delusions and hallucinations
  • People with disorganized schizophrenia often have difficulty expressing themselves

Type of Schizophrenia
Residual schizophrenia

  • You may be diagnosed with residual schizophrenia if you have a history of psychosis
  • but only experience the negative symptoms (such as slow movement, poor memory, lack of concentration and poor hygiene).

Type of Schizophrenia
Catatonic schizophrenia

  • This is the rarest schizophrenia diagnosis
  • characterized by unusual, limited and sudden movements
  • switches between being very active or very still
  • may not talk much, and you may mimic others' speech and movement

why treat schizophrenia?
Possible issues with untreated Schizophrenia

  • Can cause considerable suffering, distress and bafflement to the patient
  • higher risk of suicide, aggression and drug abuse

why treat schizophrenia?
The aim of treatment for Schizophrenia

  • Help patients toward normal interactions with others
  • Enable patients to live in the community
  • Controlling the illness through the smallest effective dosage of medication. A combination of medication and psychotherapy is usually required

Treatments for Schizophrenia
medication

  • Schizophrenia is often due to abnormal biochemical balances in the brain
  • medication can help to normalize these imbalances
  • medication is known as antipsychotics or neuroleptics
  • relieve the hallucinations, delusions and thinking problems or even eliminating the chances of relapse

Treatments for Schizophrenia
Psychotherapy

  • Helps the person make sense of their illness
  • can happen in a one-to-one basis or in a group setting
  • Issues that may need to be dealt with include: feelings about the illness, experience of medication, and other aspects of his/her life
